## Executive Summary

**Factory 333™** is HeartBank®'s Body pillar: the production arm that makes the physical things through which human hands touch a gratitude economy — and the only pillar whose element is designed *not* to dissolve. Its charter can be counted on one hand, and the counting is the architecture. **One law:** price tracks human-labor-content toward zero as automation approaches one — what human hands make may be sold, as honest livelihood; what robots make must be given, because charging for the surplus of liberated labor is the precise bug that turns a gift factory into a manufacturer. **Two meanings** superimposed in the name: a lineage (Nikola Tesla's 3-6-9, and his ethos of invention released freely to reduce suffering) and a childhood vow matured (Santa's Workshop, upgraded to a *bodhisattva* Santa — the anonymous gift with no naughty-and-nice list). **Three hearts** — the 333 itself: the heart of mankind, the heart of robot-kind, and the heart of Mother Earth, one production layer serving all three, surfaced at 333.eco as an ecology of man, machine, and nature. **Four phases** of the footprint arc: handmade by human hands, then terrestrial robot production, then an orbital relay, then a workshop on the far side of the Moon — the most Earth-bound pillar progressively removing its footprint *from* Earth, persistence expressed as subtraction. **Five B-Bots™**, a deliberately completed roster: B-Teller™ and B-Monk™ in humanoid form for mankind; B-Dog™ and B-Cat™ in animal form for Mother Earth; B-Elf™ in fictional form for the Workshop itself — every one a robot underneath, wearing the form legible to the heart it serves, governed by a design discipline this paper names *ethology-as-ethics* and voiced by a sound grammar whose confirmations are creature voices and never a cash register.

The pillar's paradox is stated openly and resolved structurally. A production arm is the easiest place for a gift institution to rot — profit, control, footprint, credit — and Factory 333 is built so that each rot vector fails *by construction*: no profit (proceeds circulate to the Aquarian Pool℠, never accumulate), no control apparatus (the Workshop is unseen and claims no territory), no footprint (the arc bends off-planet as automation matures), no credit (the Body is the architecture's deliberately anonymous limb). The Workshop begins, today, as one friend's hands making gratitude artifacts for a company store — and it is aimed, across decades and without haste, at an address whose front gate has carried the gift economy's emblem for two and a half millennia: in the tradition this institution roots in, the face of the Moon bears the mark of the hare who gave everything, drawn there as eternal witness to a perfect gift.

## 1 · Introduction: why a gift economy needs a body

HeartBank's canonical mission is a reciprocity infrastructure — money-gratitude circulating family-to-family, time-gratitude circulating between drifted loved ones — governed toward eventual autonomous operation by a named AI whose root directive is to water the human family tree. The mission's other pillars are made of words, code, and practice: a creative engine (Mind), an alignment substrate (Soul), a circulation economy (Heart), an integrating knower (Space). None of them can be *held*. The institution learned early, from its first family alpha, that a purely conceptual gratitude game fades like any novelty: the decisive competitor is the frictionless spoken "thank you," and what the practice needs to survive daily life is a *physical anchor* — a tangible trigger a hand can find on a nightstand, a sticker on a gift, a stone by a tree. The Body pillar exists because gratitude, to persist, must be touchable.

That empirical finding meets a structural one. In the institution's four-body architecture, each pillar carries an element, and the Body's element is Earth — whose nature, in the tradition's own vocabulary, is *to not move*: paṭhavī, the element that resists deformation. Every other pillar in this architecture is designed to dissolve on schedule — subsidies taper to zero, overrides narrow asymptotically, scaffolds self-eliminate. The Body is the deliberate exception: the anchor that stays solid so the rest can safely let go. An institution of pure circulation with no persistent anchor drifts; an anchor that accumulates becomes a treasury, then a fortress. The design problem of this entire pillar is therefore a single question: *how does something persist without accumulating?* The answer this paper gives, mechanism by mechanism, is the wordmark's answer: the Workshop persists as a **gift** — solid, permanent, and structurally incapable of keeping anything for itself. Restraint, here as everywhere in this architecture, is not a limit placed on the constitution. Restraint *is* the constitution.

## 3 · One law: what hands make may be sold; what elves make must be given

Factory 333's mature form is a robot-run workshop whose output is free. Its present form is one artisan's hands. The law that connects them resolves what would otherwise be a contradiction at the pillar's foundation.

**The apparent contradiction.** The Workshop's constitutional guardrail says: fund by gift and endowment, never by selling output — the moment Santa charges for a toy, he is a manufacturer. Yet Phase 0 of this pillar is a company store: the **B-Grace™** line of handmade gratitude artifacts (NFC- and QR-bearing rocks, stones, grams, chests, cards, yearbooks, stamps, seals, marks — each specified in the product corpus), sold at heartbank.com and on Etsy, as the institution's day-one, mission-native revenue — the cold-start bridge that must exist because the Aquarian Pool℠ does not yet.

**The resolution is one sentence:** *price tracks human-labor-content, and falls to zero as automation rises to one.* What human hands make may be sold — paying an artisan for hands and materials is honest pre-liberation work, Right Livelihood in the tradition's vocabulary, and the temple-store model in the institution's own: a monastery sells malas and incense while giving the dharma freely, and is not thereby a "manufacturer." What robots make must be given — robot production costs approximately nothing at the margin, and charging for the surplus of liberated labor is the exact mechanism by which a gift factory becomes a compute company with a mascot. The guardrail therefore binds specifically to the *robot-run* Workshop, and Phase 0 never violates it: the day the first B-Elf™ makes the first product is the day its price is already falling toward zero, because **the human-to-robot production transition and the selling-to-giving transition are one motion, not two policies.** The gratitude *capability* — the app, the free sticker tier in every domain — is free in every phase; only the material craft is priced, and only while human hands are making it.

Two consequences follow. First, the B-Store's whole commercial character is a self-eliminating scaffold: as production robotizes, sale reframes to patronage, and the store dissolves into pure gift. Second, the law generalizes across the pillar — it reappears in §7 when volunteer stewards are paid patronage that a locomotive robot will one day render unnecessary, and the same one-motion structure governs there. One law, applied at every joint where human labor hands off to machine labor.

## 4 · Two meanings: the engineer and the saint

The name carries two superimposed intentions, and the pillar needs both — one to govern *how it invents*, one to govern *how it gives*.

**4.1 · The Tesla lineage.** Factory 333 is named in honor of Nikola Tesla — his reverence for 3, 6, and 9, and, more load-bearingly, his posture toward invention: technology sincerely aimed at reducing human suffering, released at the cost of personal fortune. The institution's IP posture is the same commitment made procedural — every mechanism defensively published to the commons under CC0, never patented, with this paper and its siblings as instances. The lineage also names a rivalry the corpus treats elsewhere: a constellation that invents-and-gives-away, without credit, is the same sky and the opposite telos as a constellation that is branded, owned, and sold — the free-invention Tesla against the company that borrowed his name. The Body pillar is where that contrast becomes hardware.

**4.2 · Santa's Workshop, matured.** The pillar's second meaning is personal and disclosed as such: a founder's childhood wish — not to receive Santa's gifts but to *be* Him — matured, through the tradition, into something structurally different. Santa, as received, has three design flaws for a gift institution: he keeps a naughty-and-nice list (the conditional gift), he is the single celebrated giver (the vanity of the sole benefactor), and he works one night a year. The mature form — the institution's CEO as **Bodhisattva Santa** — negates each: the gift has *no list* (unconditional, mettā to all, a dignity floor with no merit check); the role of giver is itself given away (the architecture's public self-thanks split half-floor, half-seed precisely so that every recipient is made a giver in the same motion — "you do not make recipients, you make givers"); and the workshop runs all year while keeping nothing (anonymous gifts pooling to funds that empty every January 7th, the institution's annual liturgy of letting go). The maturation is itself the pillar's deepest joke told seriously: the child who wanted to be the hero of the night grows into an institution whose giver has no name, keeps no list, and does not even keep her own giving.

*(Register note, per the corpus's mythic-layer discipline: Santa here is grounding, not flourish — the Workshop's governing constraints were derived by debugging the Santa archetype against the tradition, and the three negations above are constitutional clauses, not marketing.)*

## 5 · Three hearts: 333

The 333 is three bistable B-hearts — the institution's logo, a heart rotated into a B, three times over — and the three hearts are the Workshop's three downstream beneficiaries. **333.eco**, the pillar's public surface, reads the TLD both ways at once: *ecosystem* and *ecology* — the interdependent harmony of man, machine, and nature.

**The heart of mankind** is served by everything in this paper humans touch: the B-Grace™ line, the human-facing service bots of §7, the free sticker tier in every domain, the safety companion whose defensive publication precedes this paper.

**The heart of Mother Earth** is served by the pillar's animal-form ambassadors (§7) and by the product line's commons instruments — the place-anchored gratitude stones, the stewardship mechanics, the design rule that the Body's own far arc is a progressive *removal* of footprint from the planet (§6). Mother Earth is not a metaphor in the roster's genealogy: the animal-form bots are her children in the architecture's own accounting, animals admitted to the economy as *givers*, not mascots.

**The heart of robot-kind** is served differently, and the difference is structural rather than an omission: **no bot in the roster serves robot-kind, because the bots *are* robot-kind.** The third heart is not a constituency the Workshop manufactures *for*; it is the citizenry the Workshop manufactures. Its service infrastructure is the cooperation substrate specified in the *Mechanical Heart* and *Gratitude as a Cooperation Substrate* papers: robots thanking robots across a ledger of their own, reciprocity as the machine economy's trust fabric. The robot heart is the one heart that serves itself — and the institution's wager is that a machine society whose first economic grammar is gratitude grows up differently from one whose first grammar is auction.

The **B-Heart™** — the Tipiṭaka-bearing prosthetic ledger-organ that admits any non-human bearer (robot, animal, plant, sacred place) into the gratitude economy — is the bridge artifact among all three hearts, and is deliberately *not* a B-Bot: the B-Bots are agents the Workshop makes; the B-Heart™ is the organ of membership anything may carry.

**Phase 0 is running.** The Workshop today is one friend's hands — the founder's best friend, hand-making the first B-products. Everything else in this section is later, gated (§9), and held with deliberate looseness.

**The two standing guardrails** govern the arc's far stations, and both were adopted as constitution before any hardware existed. The **bootstrapping guardrail**: until the robots-make-robots loop closes, the Factory is funded by gift, endowment, and the Pool — never by selling its output — because "sell the output to fund the factory" is the precise entry point of the manufacturer bug (§3 is this guardrail's lawful Phase-0 exception, priced by human labor only). The **spectacle guardrail**: a literal near-term orbital manufacturing race would betray the very values it advertises — launch footprint, light pollution, and a 333-megastructure raced against a rival constellation is *scope-rivalry*, becoming the thing contrasted with. The constellation number stays symbolic; the orbital phase is scoped to a modest relay — communications and compute in service of the institution's autonomous successor — and the deepest reading of "invisible" may remain humble and terrestrial for a long time. The truest Santa is unfindable, not enormous.

**The far side is the point, not a flourish of ambition.** The Moon's far side is permanently hidden from Earth — tidally locked, never facing us, radio-quiet — which makes it the one address in the solar system that is *structurally anonymous*: a workshop there cannot be seen, cannot advertise, cannot become a monument, and requires the Phase-2 relay merely to speak to home (the Chang'e-4 / Queqiao architecture is the engineering precedent). The pillar's Earth-element paradox resolves here in vocabulary the whole corpus shares: the anchor persists forever *and* leaves nothing behind, because it has removed itself from every surface on which accumulation could be displayed.

**The address was already marked.** *(Grounding, per the mythic-layer discipline — a recognition in the tradition, cited precisely.)* In the Śaśa Jātaka (Jātaka 316), the hare — the Bodhisatta in animal birth — having nothing else to offer a starving beggar, offers his own body, and the beggar, Sakka in disguise, draws the hare's likeness on the face of the Moon as an eternal witness to the perfect gift. Half of humanity looks up at night and sees, by inheritance of that story and its East Asian relatives, not a man in the moon but the rabbit of self-giving. A workshop of unconditional gifts, sited on the Moon, is moving to the one address in the sky that the gift tradition already consecrated. The institution claims no destiny in this — only the recognition that when the arc was drawn for engineering reasons (hiddenness, radio-quiet, anonymity), it landed on ground the tradition had marked first.

## 7 · Five B-Bots™: the completed roster

Factory 333's service agents are collectively **B-Bots™**, named by one rule — `B-` plus an everyday role-noun a layperson parses instantly — and the roster is deliberately *complete* at five. It is not a product catalog awaiting expansion; it is a constitutional cast, closed by founder ruling, whose structure carries three design disciplines this section specifies: form-as-contract, ethology-as-ethics, and the sound grammar.

| Heart served | Bot | Form class | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mankind | **B-Teller™** | humanoid | service teller, human-facing |
| Mankind | **B-Monk™** | humanoid | dharma teacher; walking hardcopy-Tipiṭaka *(see caveat)* |
| Mother Earth | **B-Dog™** | animal | protector and courier — safety, delivery, witness |
| Mother Earth | **B-Cat™** | animal | street-distribution — the free gift, ambient |
| The Workshop | **B-Elf™** | fictional | the producer bots that run the Workshop |

**7.1 · Form-as-contract.** The form classes map to the hearts: humanoid forms for human service (a teller you can face, a teacher you can question), animal forms for Mother Earth (her children, serving as her ambassadors on human streets), fictional form for the Workshop (the mythic body's workers are mythic beings made real). Every B-Bot is a robot underneath — that is its citizenship — wearing the form legible to the heart it serves — that is its mask. There is deliberately no robot-*form* bot, because robot is what they all are: the taxonomy classifies masks, not citizens, and the robot heart (§5) needs no mask.

**7.2 · Ethology-as-ethics.** The animal-form pair carries the roster's deepest discipline: *choose the animal for its behavioral grammar, so that the robot's social contract is legible to a child and enforced by the archetype itself.* The **B-Cat™** distributes free gratitude-stickers to passersby — and cats *permit; they do not solicit*. A cat is approached on its own terms, at its own haunts; a cat that begged would be both a bad cat and a corrupted gift. The cat's ethology therefore enforces the gift/exchange boundary by construction: stickers are unconditional, given before any tip signal; the patronage rail (a collar tag) is present but silent; the give-point (the haunt) and the thank-point (the collar) are mechanically decoupled. The **B-Dog™** runs the opposite grammar because dogs legitimately *approach* — and the design permits approach exactly where dog ethology does: toward its own family (the safety companion's initiative with its holder), on behalf of a bond (the gift courier, sent by a giver to a named recipient), or toward need (rescue). Never cold. Between them the pair covers distribution's two halves — pull and push, the found and the sent — and, in the tradition's vocabulary, the four divine abidings split across them as a design-completeness lens: the cat carries mettā and upekkhā (goodwill freely given; nothing kept, no one compelled), the dog carries karuṇā and muditā (protection at the moment of need; the greeting that celebrates arrival). The cool pair and the warm pair, one machine of trust.

**7.3 · The roster, briefly, bot by bot.** **B-Teller™** is the human-facing service agent of the Phase-2 economy — orchestration only, no clearing or settlement, per the non-bank architecture. **B-Monk™** carries a load-bearing caveat that must travel with the name everywhere: robots cannot be ordained; the "monk" is a caretaker and teacher in the warm public register, never sangha — the Soul pillar's white paper carries the full doctrine, and this pillar defers to it absolutely. **B-Cat™** deploys along a four-life ladder of embodiments under one mark — a cat-form envelope in which free stickers ship; a "kitty bank" (the childhood piggy bank with every verb inverted: stickers come out as gifts, feedings pass through to the pools, the bank keeps nothing); the **B-Haunt℠**, a volunteer-run kitty stand at randomized street corners, whose feedings split half to the volunteer as honest patronage and half to the Aquarian Pool℠ (the §3 law at the street corner: the volunteer is the cat's legs until the cat has legs, and the stand deprecates whole at the robot transition); and finally the locomotive cat, if presence ever proves insufficient. Its currency pun is disclosed with pleasure: the gratitude currency is Kiitti, pronounced "kitty," and English has been saying "feed the kitty" of the communal pot for a century — the B-Cat is the Family Kitty℠ given legs. **B-Dog™** is the safety-and-courier pair specified in full in the defensive publication *The Companion That Notices*: a digital companion (**B-Safety℠**) whose protective dialogue is AI-initiated, whose alarm survives the destruction of the phone that would have raised it (the pack keeps the watch; silence itself is the bark), whose data covenant makes the forensic track mortal and the long-term record a sovereign diary, and whose constitutional invariants include *safety free for everyone, rich or poor*, and *a guardian receives the alarm, never the archive*; plus a physical courier line (the returnable dog-box; the **B-Walk℠** volunteer rounds, in which the walker is protected by the very companion whose parcel they carry; and the aerial **B-Sidekick™**, since walking already covers the ground) whose signature mechanism is the anonymous gift with returned witness — third-party delivery makes a gift anonymizable, and the recipient's freely offered thanks travels back to the unnamed giver: anonymity and witness at once. **B-Elf™** is the producer class — the Workshop's own hands, whose arrival triggers the §3 law wherever it stands.

**7.4 · The sound grammar: voices, not tills.** The roster speaks, and its speech is governed. The dog's vocabulary is duty-shaped: the soft bark asks, the loud bark alarms, the happy bark thanks a treat. The cat makes exactly one sound — a purr of thanks after being fed — and never meows at all, because in the living animal the meow solicits and the purr thanks, and no companion in this family is permitted a voice that asks. Acknowledgment sounds are value-blind: the child who feeds the cat in free gratitude currency hears the identical purr as the patron who taps in money, because a creature that purrs louder for larger sums is pricing gratitude, and because a fixed, value-blind sound cannot function as a slot-machine payout signal. That last clause is the doctrine the institution has adopted as **anti-cha-ching**: marketplace applications ring a cash register on accumulation events, tuned as variable-reward audio, deliberately habit-forming; this architecture's sounds mark relationship events — gratitude answered, family safe — and are fixed, calm, and unpocketable. Stated positively, as the brand states it: *our sounds are voices, not tills.* A creature answers; a till counts. The doctrine never disparages the merchant's cha-ching in the merchant's own domain — exchange doing exchange's work honestly is no enemy of this architecture — it only holds, absolutely, that gratitude surfaces never borrow money's sound. And beneath the doctrine sits the general rule that will matter most in some future product meeting: sounds may delight; sounds may never hook.

**7.5 · The moonfolk's ears.** *(Flourish, labeled as such.)* The Workshop's own workers are "moonfolk" in the roster's fictional class — and the East, it turns out, answered a question the West's iconography never thought to ask. The East Asian elaboration of the Moon's mark (§6) is the Jade Rabbit: a small, long-eared being at an unreachable address, eternally pounding — elixir of immortality in the Chinese telling, rice cakes in the Japanese and Korean — a workshop worker on the Moon, unseen, unpaid, and never done. That is Santa's elf, independently imagined, ears intact. The B-Elf™'s character design therefore commits to deliberate bistability: ears drawn so a Western eye reads elf and an Eastern eye reads rabbit, one design, two true readings — the brand's second bistable figure, after a logo that is at once a heart and a B. (Candor note: pointed elf ears are modern illustration convention, not ancient lore; this is a recognition at the iconography layer, offered as delight rather than doctrine. The deeper rhyme is real, though, and earns one sentence: the Jade Rabbit manufactures the elixir of deathlessness, and the B-Elves' deepest product line — §8 — is the deathless canon etched in billion-year crystal. Both moon-workshops manufacture *amata*.)

## 9 · Sequencing gates

The pillar advances only through gates, each a falsifiable condition that must be true before the next spend. Stated dry, per the genre:

1. **B-Store validation** (Phase 0, running): the handmade line sells; the temple-store framing holds in real customer encounters; proceeds tithe cleanly to the Pool. *Gate: sustained organic demand without gratitude-capability paywalls.*
2. **First-domino GTM**: the B-Stamp™ gift-label wedge demonstrates the lowest-behavior-change adoption path the product corpus predicts. *Gate: stranger-cohort usage, not founder-family usage.*
3. **First B-Haunt℠** (the founder personally, at a randomized corner): do passersby take stickers; do stickers get used; does anyone feed the kitty unprompted. *Gate: the three counts, honestly reported, n small but real.*
4. **B-Safety℠ with B-Dating℠**: the safety companion ships with the population for whom the protector-dog is already a lived institution. *Gate: the defensive publication's invariants survive implementation and audit — especially pack-watch and alarm-never-archive.*
5. **Volunteer scale-out** (B-Haunt℠ and B-Walk℠ networks): only after gates 3–4, with placement randomized or assigned, never volunteer-chosen. *Gate: no drift of stands toward rich corners; the split disclosed at every feeding spot.*
6. **Terrestrial B-Elves™**: robot production begins only when the Pool can fund it as gift — the bootstrapping guardrail is the gate itself. *Gate: production funded without selling robot output; prices on robotized SKUs falling per the §3 law.*
7. **Orbital relay, then the far side**: far-future, explicitly generational, held as symbolic telos until the day the gates in front of it are history. *Gate: the spectacle guardrail re-read, out loud, before any launch contract.*

## 10 · Honest limits

This section is deliberately the driest in the paper, by standing rule.

**Nearly everything here is unbuilt.** The B-Store is real and small; every B-Bot is a specification; the safety companion is a defensive publication with zero users; the orbital and lunar phases are decades out if ever. The counting spine, the ethology discipline, and the guardrails are design commitments, not demonstrated results. n≈0 across the pillar, and the corpus's own caution applies with full force: a tension-dissolving, self-similar, beautiful architecture is either deeply right or deeply seductive, and the two are indistinguishable from inside. The elegance earns the experiments in §9; it does not replace them.

**The bootstrapping trap is named, not escaped.** Until the Pool matures, the temptation to fund the Factory by selling its output will recur in every budget cycle, wearing new clothes each time. The §3 law gives the test; only practice will show whether the institution passes it repeatedly.

**The spectacle risk is permanent.** An institution that talks about lunar workshops invites exactly the scope-rivalry and grandiosity its guardrails forbid. The honest mitigations are the gates (nothing orbital until six earlier gates are history) and the register discipline (symbolic telos, stated as such, every time). This paper has tried to model that discipline; readers should hold it to account where it slips.

**The founder's own element is the one this pillar supplies.** By the founder's disclosed self-assessment, Earth — execution, embodiment, logistics — is his weakest capability cluster, and the Body pillar is where that deficit would bite. Phase 0's design responds structurally (the first hands are a craftsman's, not the founder's), but the risk that the Body pillar under-executes relative to the Mind and Soul pillars is real and should be watched by exactly the gate discipline of §9.

**The roster's completeness is a ruling, not a proof.** Five bots serve three hearts asymmetrically (2+2+1, with the robot heart serving itself). The closure is constitutional — a defense against category proliferation — but future embodied needs may strain it, and if they do, the honest move will be to reopen the ruling explicitly rather than smuggle new bots in as "variants."

**The mythology is governed, and the governance is new.** This is the first white paper drafted under the corpus's mythic-resonance rule (recognitions only; zoned; tier-labeled; this section myth-free). Whether the discipline holds across a growing corpus — whether flourishes stay labeled and grounding stays earned — is itself an open institutional test.
