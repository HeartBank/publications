## Executive Summary

The controversy over AI-made art has one honest core: generative models are distilled from humanity's collective creative output, and their outputs are being enclosed — priced, licensed, and sold back to the very commons they were made from. The standard defenses (fair use, transformation, inevitability) answer the law and dodge the grievance. The institution's position answers the grievance: **the fault in AI art was never the receiving — it was the enclosing.** Artificial intelligence is the culmination of humanity's collective outputs; therefore what is made with it is owed back to everyone. The only business model that fully answers the enclosure critique is the gift: **AI-made works released freely to all, with revenue earned beside the gift, never from it** — patronage of the making, honored with premiere and credits; tools rented to creators; thanks received after the fact, freely given. The institution does not merely argue this position; it is building the mechanism — **B-Studio℠** (the workshop), **B-Sala™** (the venue where only gifts play), **B-Premiere℠** (the patronage event) — and binding its own studio to the model first.

## 1. The controversy, stated fairly

The case against AI-made art deserves to be stated at full strength, because the institution accepts its premise. Generative models are trained on the accumulated writing, image-making, music, and story of every culture — overwhelmingly without the consent of the people who made it. Their outputs now compete against those same people, in their own markets, at near-zero marginal cost. The value of the collective inheritance is captured at two points — the model layer and the content layer — and at both points it is enclosed: proprietary weights, subscription outputs, monetized feeds. The artists' grievance, reduced to a sentence, is: *you made that from us, and now you are selling it back to us.* The standard industry defenses do not answer this sentence. Fair-use arguments answer courts. Transformation arguments answer philosophers. Inevitability arguments answer no one. The sentence stands.

## 2. The position: the fault was the enclosing, not the receiving

The institution's answer concedes the premise and reverses the conclusion. Yes — artificial intelligence is the culmination of humanity's collective outputs; that is not the scandal, it is the *ground*. Every human creator also makes from the inheritance: every painter from every painting seen, every songwriter from every song heard. Receiving from the commons is what culture *is*. The scandal begins at the second step — when what was received from everyone is enclosed against everyone. It follows that the critique of AI art cannot be answered by better licenses, better lawsuits, or better disclaimers. It can only be answered by **removing the enclosure**: works made with AI, given to everyone, permanently. *We take from everyone's inheritance only what we return to everyone.* A creator who works this way holds clean hands not by exception or by permission, but by construction — and this is available to any creator, of any scale, who accepts the premise. The position generalizes because the inheritance belongs to everyone.

## 3. The model, specified: the two-rail economy

A gift is not a business model until the making is funded. The institution's model runs on two rails, and their separation is the whole design: **value flows to creators untaxed; infrastructure is funded beside the gift, never from it.**

**Rail one — the creator's income.** Works are financed by **patronage of the making**: supporters pledge toward a work that does not yet exist — in the institution's grammar, a *Thank Your Future Kindness℠* pledge, gratitude aimed forward — and are honored as givers: names in the credits as on a temple wall, invitation to the private premiere as an honor extended, never a price of admission. **The free public release date is committed before a single premiere seat is offered**, so the gift is a promise rather than a leftover and no revenue pressure can extend the window. After release, the thanks channel opens: whoever the work moves may tip — after receipt, freely, in gratitude's native direction. And at the afterglow, the invitation extends forward: patronage of the *next* work — never framed as repayment (a gift repaid becomes a debt, and the gift is not a debt) but as joining the giving side. Each work given away completely; each work's afterglow kindling the next. The platform takes **no percentage of any of it, ever.**

**Rail two — the infrastructure's income.** The platform earns by renting what the studio had to build anyway: the advanced AI production toolchain, offered as a premium tier of the creator's workshop. The rule that keeps this rail honest: **premium prices the making, never the giving.** Heavy AI generation is genuinely costly, so the scale of what a creator can *generate* tracks the tier — an honest cost line. But publishing is never gated: any finished work lists freely, at any length, at full quality. A creator never pays to give.

## 4. The mechanism: B-Studio℠, B-Sala™, B-Premiere℠

The position is not an aspiration; it is an architecture under construction. **B-Studio℠** is the creator's workshop — every creator has one, from a grandmother recording a thank-you to a filmmaker building a feature; the free tier suffices to create, publish, and run the full patronage circuit; premium adds the advanced toolchain. **B-Sala™** is the venue, named for the open-sided hall of every Khmer temple — a building that cannot charge admission because it has no walls: *only gifts play here*, by certification (committed free release, no paywall) and by curation (no advertising, no algorithmic feed — programmed like a festival, never scrolled like a feed). **B-Premiere℠** is the patronage event where givers are honored. Creators are verified humans with disclosed provenance; listings are curated and revocable — the gift framing does not filter quality by itself, so the venue has a programmer, not an upload button. The institution's own studio at THonly™ is simply the first B-Studio, bound to the model before anyone else is asked to trust it, with the fairy-tale musical *Apsara Princess* — a film for a country whose cinema was interrupted — as the first work: under this model, a Khmer child never pays to see the Khmer princess, and a Khmer creator never pays to publish.

## 5. What this position does not claim

Honesty about the position's edges. **It does not solve individual compensation for the trained-upon.** The gift model returns value to the commons in aggregate — everyone receives the works — but it does not pay royalties to the specific artists whose work trained the models; the attribution-and-compensation problem remains open industry-wide, and this position answers the *enclosure* charge, not that one. **It does not declare commercial AI art illegitimate.** The claim of the title is scoped: the gift is the only model that *fully answers the enclosure critique* — the only one under which the sentence of §1 has no target — not the only model anyone may operate. **It is published before it is proven.** The studio has released no film yet; the venue is not open; the sequencing is deliberate — the studio's own works must prove the circuit before other creators are invited into it — and this document exists so the position is on the record, dated, before the proof. **And the tools rail has a half-life.** Frontier labs will eventually commoditize production tooling; the subscription is bridge revenue. The institution expects that erosion and states it now: tooling advantages expire; postures compound. What compounds here is the venue, the norm, and the community of creators with clean hands.

## 6. Why this institution holds this position

Because it is the same position the institution holds everywhere. HeartBank®'s wordmark doctrine is *circulation, not accumulation*; its creative brand adopts Disney's architecture and inverts Disney's enclosure; its research corpus is dedicated to the public domain; its moat is what it refuses. The gift model for AI-made art is that single posture, applied at the training-data layer — and it is the layer where the posture matters most, because AI is the largest act of receiving-from-the-commons in human history, and the question of what is owed back is the question of the age. The institution's answer, in the form of a working studio, a wall-less venue, and a creator economy that never taxes a gift: **everything.**

## Closing

The controversy over AI-made art is not going away, because its core sentence is true. There is exactly one way to make that sentence lose its target — give the works back to the everyone they came from — and it happens to produce, as a side effect, the first creator economy in which the audience is never the product, the work is never the hostage, and the artist is never the toll collector. The institution commits its own studio to this model first, invites every creator who shares the premise to the same clean hands, and offers the position itself, like everything else, to the commons.
