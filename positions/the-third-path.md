## Executive Summary

The political economy of giving has long been argued between two poles. On one, **coerced redistribution** corrects the distribution from above, by compulsory transfer. On the other, **pure accumulation** preserves liberty over holdings and never asks for a transfer at all. HeartBank's institutional position is that there is a third position, orthogonal to both, that neither pole can occupy: **voluntary circulation** — relinquishment freely chosen, measured, and mirrored back to the giver.

The position rests on one load-bearing claim: *the relinquishment HeartBank cultivates cannot be coerced.* A gift compelled from outside produces the transfer without the free volition that makes a gift a gift — and, by a well-established body of evidence on motivation, compulsion and payment tend to *crowd out* the very disposition they are meant to support. It follows that the institution can only ever provide the **arena** in which the free choice is available and the **mirror** by which a participant reads their own progress — never the lever. And it follows that HeartBank requires a host society that leaves the disposal of one's own holdings free, because the free choice not to accumulate is virtuous, and measurable, only where accumulation was genuinely available. The institution is therefore the voluntary-circulation layer a free order structurally lacks: not a redistributive mechanism, not a neutral marketplace, but the valve that completes a free order from within.

## 1. The two poles

Coerced redistribution achieves a distribution but cannot grow a disposition: a transfer extracted by compulsion is not generosity, however just the resulting pattern. Pure accumulation preserves liberty but offers no valve: care remains a private surplus, expressed if at all by individual whim, with no infrastructure to make the giving easy, dignified, or visible-to-oneself. Each pole is answering a real concern of the other — distribution, or liberty — and neither addresses the thing HeartBank exists to grow: the freely-chosen, cultivated practice of circulation.

## 2. The third position

HeartBank is the voluntary-circulation layer. It does not compel a transfer (the redistributive move) and it does not merely host a market (the accumulation move). It provides an arena in which giving is structurally easy and a mirror in which a participant can see their own movement from holding toward circulating — and then it leaves the choice entirely free. It completes a free order rather than replacing it, by adding from within, voluntarily, the circulation that order does not supply on its own.

## 3. Why it cannot be coerced

The claim that the relinquishment cannot be coerced is not sentiment; it is structural. What the institution values is a cultivated interior disposition — generosity freely chosen — and a coerced relinquishment yields the outward fact (the transfer) while voiding the interior one (the free volition). The contemplative analysis the institution's substrate carries is explicit on this: it is the volition that makes an act meritorious, not the transfer alone. And the empirical literature on motivation converges: paying for or compelling prosocial behavior frequently *crowds out* the intrinsic disposition, converting a relation of care into a priced or policed transaction (the classic findings on paid versus voluntary blood donation, and on fines that became prices, are the canonical cases). Coercion does not approximate the target imperfectly; it destroys the thing while producing its shadow.

## 4. What this means for the host society

If the relinquishment must be free, the institution requires a host in which *not* relinquishing is a live, real option — because the virtue and the measurement both depend on the choice being free. This is a narrow claim and the institution states it narrowly, against the loose version that would misread it. HeartBank does **not** hold that any particular accumulation order is good, that inequality should be endorsed, or that redistribution is wrong. It holds only that a society leaving the disposal of one's own holdings free is the necessary *arena* for the cultivated, measurable relinquishment the institution grows. Virtue measured is virtue uncompelled.

## 5. The architecture already encodes it

The position is not aspirational; it is built. The institution rule-empties its **transient communal vessels** — the earmarked re-tip pools that exist to be passed onward — on a fixed annual cadence; but this is not coercion, because depositing into such a vessel *is itself the free act*, and the emptying only enforces a commitment the participant already made. The institution **never** rule-empties a participant's **personal account**: its return toward zero is left entirely to the participant's free circulation. Freedom applies to what is yours; the institution may hold a freely-made commitment to its purpose, but it may never seize a holding. The line between the two vessels is the third path made structural.

## Closing

HeartBank's position is that the most important transfers cannot be commanded into existence and should not be left to chance — they must be *invited*, made easy, and reflected back to the giver, in a society that leaves the giver free to refuse. That is the third path: not the state's compulsion and not the market's silence, but the freely-chosen circulation that completes a free order from within. The institution builds the arena and holds up the mirror, and then — even at the cost of a lower number — it leaves the choice free.
